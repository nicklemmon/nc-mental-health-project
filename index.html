What is mental health treatment?

Getting care starts with learning what to ask for

In the current system, level of care is based on a client's need. The following is a list of the most common services in North Carolina. This is not an exhaustive list, but ideally, each of these services are available in every county across the state.

Outpatient therapy
Traditional talk therapy in an office-based setting. Therapy is an effective tool for helping clients build the skills needed to manage independently on a day-to-day basis. There is state-funding to pay for this service for uninsured residents of North Carolina, but the number of sessions is limited.
Outpatient medication management
Typically the role of psychiatry, medication management often complements other services through the prescription of medication. Medication offers symptom relief which can help promote stability, and ideally, recovery. There is also state funding for this service for uninsured residents, though sessions tend to be brief and with loq frequency.
Psychosocial rehabilitation
PSR for short, these programs offer a form of social structure through a local clubhouse model. Typically these programs are reserved for individuals with serious mental illness, PSR offers a community-based alternative for individuals who may have previously been institutionalized.
Assertive Community Treatment Team (ACTT)
ACTT is the highest level of community-based care and typically reserved for clients with serious mental illness and resulting functional impairments. These teams are made up of social workers, psychiatrists, nurses, and other qualified professionals who help clients maintain stability by providing wraparound services.

What does the system look like?

Learn the lay of the land

Decentralized funding

North Carolina uses a model of managed care which funds community mental health services for state residents who have no health insurance and for those with Medicaid. The State uses Medicaid funding and general revenue to fund regional Managed Care Organizations (MCO).

Each MCO operates slightly differently, however, all have centralized intake systems to help connect clients with care.

MCOs then contract with local non-profit and for-profit mental health providers in order to provide services directly. Most clients, consumers, and family members never know about the Managed Care Organizations that fund their local therapist and psychiatrist.

Managed Care Organizations (MCOs)

Around 15 years ago, there were many, small MCOs across the state, each operating within a relatively small geographic area. Some of these organizations were better than others, resulting in a wide variety with regards to quality of care. As time has passed, the number of these organizations has decreased, while the remaining organizations become larger.

Funding mental health providers

Regional MCOs use their state funding to pay local mental health providers to provide services. Mental health providers then treat patients, and get reimbursed through these funds. Unfortunately, this funding is only for use with people who have Medicaid and for people who have no insurance. If you or a family member needs services and has Medicare or private insurance, read here to see what steps should be taken to get connected to care.

What about Medicare or private insurance?

Different funding with different options

Medicare

If you have Medicare Part A, you already use your insurance to see doctors at outpatient clinics for primary care and other specialties. Mental health treatment works the same way; with outpatient coverage you can search for providers through Medicare.gov.

Be prepared! Typically Medicare requires co-payments for outpatient appointments, so call ahead before the first appointment to find out about out-of-pocket costs.

Unfortunately, Medicare does not pay for enhanced services such as ACTT or PSR. If these services are needed, contact your local Managed Care Organization (MCO) and ask about state-funded enhanced services.

Private insurance

With private insurance, patients can choose from a larger variety of providers. With all the flexibility comes a lot of complexity. As a result, this site can't offer specific guidance as far as how to connect with care for every individual as there are many private insurance companies out there.

Typically, a phone number or website for your insurance company can be found on the back of your insurance card.

Like Medicare, most private insurers do not pay for enhanced services such as ACTT or PSR.

Searching for care online

Whether you're looking via Medicare.gov or your private insurer, here are a few quick tips to help your search:

  • If you are looking for medication management, try searching for psychiatrists or psychiatric nurse practitioners
  • If you are looking for outpatient therapy, try searching for licensed clinical social workers (LCSWs), licensed practicing counselors (LPCs), licensed marriage and family therapists (LMFTs), and psychologists
